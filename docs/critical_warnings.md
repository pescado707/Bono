# Critical Warnings

## Advice

These are the most common and most damaging mistakes in immigration cases. Each one can permanently close off options.

**Never sign Form I-274 (Voluntary Departure Agreement) without attorney review:**
- Voluntary departure waives your right to appeal a removal order
- It can trigger a 10-year bar to reentry if you fail to depart by the deadline
- ICE agents may present it as routine paperwork — it is not
- Even if you intend to leave, consult an attorney first

**Never miss a Notice to Appear (NTA) court date:**
- Missing an immigration court date results in an automatic in absentia removal order (INA §240(b)(5))
- You lose the right to present your case or any defense
- An in absentia order is extremely difficult to reopen — you must prove you never received notice or had an exceptional circumstance
- Check your court date at justice.gov/eoir or call 1-800-898-7180 before every scheduled date

**Never re-enter the US after a deportation order:**
- Illegal reentry after removal is a federal crime (8 U.S.C. §1326), punishable by up to 20 years imprisonment
- Triggers a 10-year, 20-year, or permanent bar to admission depending on circumstances (INA §212(a)(9))
- Even if you have a qualifying US citizen spouse or child, this bar is nearly impossible to waive

**Never make false statements to USCIS, CBP, or an immigration judge:**
- Misrepresentation of a material fact is a permanent bar to all immigration benefits (INA §212(a)(6)(C))
- This includes omitting information on forms — USCIS cross-checks extensively
- Silence is protected; lying is not

**Never miss the 1-year asylum deadline:**
- You must file Form I-589 within 1 year of your last arrival in the US (INA §208(a)(2)(B))
- The clock starts on the day you enter, not when you learn about asylum
- Exceptions exist (changed or extraordinary circumstances) but are narrowly applied and difficult to prove
- File even if your case is incomplete — a timely incomplete filing protects the deadline

**Never travel abroad on expired, pending, or revoked status:**
- Departing the US while a petition is pending (without advance parole) can abandon the application
- Traveling on a revoked visa triggers inadmissibility at re-entry
- Advance parole (Form I-131) must be approved before travel if you have a pending I-485
- 10-year bar: 1 year or more of unlawful presence + departure = 10-year bar (INA §212(a)(9)(B))
- Permanent bar: unlawful presence after prior removal + reentry attempt = permanent bar

**Never fail to respond to an RFE (Request for Evidence):**
- An unanswered RFE results in abandonment of the petition
- RFEs have strict deadlines — typically 87 days, sometimes less
- USCIS cannot grant extra time after the deadline passes
- Even a weak response is better than no response

**Never fail to update your address with USCIS:**
- All persons in removal proceedings must file Form AR-11 within 10 days of moving
- Failure to update means court notices go to the wrong address — and you are still responsible for missing them
- Update at uscis.gov/ar-11 or by calling 1-800-375-5283

**Criminal history — any conviction can be catastrophic:**
- Even misdemeanors can trigger deportability under the "crime involving moral turpitude" (CIMT) standard
- DUI with aggravating factors is often classified as an aggravated felony
- Drug offenses, domestic violence convictions, and firearms offenses trigger mandatory detention and removal
- A conviction can permanently bar naturalization or re-entry
- Consult both a criminal defense attorney AND an immigration attorney before accepting any plea deal

## Follow-up Questions

- Have you signed any document given to you by ICE or CBP — do you know what it was?
- Have you ever been deported, removed, or granted voluntary departure in the past?
- Do you have any criminal history, including arrests that did not lead to conviction?
- Have you ever left and re-entered the US after accruing any period of unlawful presence?
- Is there a pending RFE or court deadline you are aware of?
- Have you moved recently — is your current address on file with USCIS?
