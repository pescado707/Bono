# Urgent Steps

## Advice

The first actions taken in an immigration crisis often determine the outcome. Act precisely and quickly.

**If you are currently being detained or ICE is at the door:**
1. Do not open the door unless officers show a judicial warrant signed by a judge
2. State clearly through the door: "I do not consent to entry. I want to speak to a lawyer."
3. If they force entry, do not physically resist — say "I do not consent to this search"
4. Immediately invoke your right to remain silent — do not answer questions about status, origin, or history
5. Ask for a lawyer and contact your consulate as soon as you are able to make a call
6. Do not sign any document, especially Form I-274 (voluntary departure)

**If you received a Notice to Appear (NTA):**
1. Do NOT miss the court date listed — missing it results in an automatic in absentia removal order
2. Contact an immigration attorney within 24 hours of receiving the NTA
3. Check your court date at justice.gov/eoir or call 1-800-898-7180
4. Gather all identity and immigration documents immediately (see document list below)
5. If the date is imminent (less than 7 days), contact legal aid for emergency assistance

**If your visa or status is expiring soon:**
1. File an extension (I-539 for most nonimmigrants) before the I-94 expiration date
2. A timely filed I-539 grants a 240-day grace period of authorized stay while pending
3. Do not travel outside the US while an extension is pending without attorney guidance
4. If already expired, consult an attorney immediately about options — do not delay further

**If you fear deportation is imminent:**
1. Contact an immigration attorney to discuss an emergency stay of removal
2. An emergency TRO (temporary restraining order) may be filed in federal court
3. Contact your home country's consulate — they can apply diplomatic pressure and assist with documents
4. Notify trusted contacts of your situation and location

**Documents to gather immediately:**
- Passport (all pages, including expired ones)
- All US visas (even expired)
- I-94 arrival/departure record (print from i94.cbp.dhs.gov)
- Green card (I-551) or EAD card if applicable
- All I-797 USCIS approval and receipt notices
- Birth certificate and marriage certificate (if applicable)
- Any court notices, NTAs, or government letters received
- Evidence of US ties: lease, employment letter, bank statements, children's school records

**If you need to find a detained family member:**
1. Search the ICE detainee locator at locator.ice.gov
2. Call ICE at 1-888-351-4024
3. Contact a local immigration attorney or legal aid organization immediately

## Follow-up Questions

- Is this situation happening right now, or are you preparing in advance?
- Have you received any official documents — NTA, RFE, denial notice, or deportation order?
- Do you have an attorney already, or do you need help finding one?
- When does your current visa or status expire — do you know the date on your I-94?
- Are you currently inside the US or abroad?
- Are there children or dependents involved whose status is also at risk?
