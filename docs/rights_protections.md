# Rights and Protections

## Advice

All people in the United States — regardless of immigration status — have constitutional and statutory rights. Knowing them before an encounter can prevent irreversible harm.

**Fourth Amendment — searches and seizures:**
- ICE cannot enter a home without a judicial warrant signed by a federal judge
- An ICE administrative warrant (Form I-200 or I-205) is NOT a judicial warrant — you are not required to open the door
- To verify: a judicial warrant names a specific person, is signed by a judge, and cites a federal statute (e.g., 8 U.S.C.)
- Workplace raids: ICE may enter public areas but cannot search private offices without consent or a warrant
- If ICE enters without a warrant and you did not consent, tell your attorney — evidence may be suppressed

**Fifth Amendment — right to remain silent:**
- You do not have to answer questions from ICE, CBP, or any immigration officer about your status, origin, or history
- Clearly state: "I am exercising my right to remain silent" — then stop talking
- Do not lie; silence is protected but false statements are a permanent bar (INA §212(a)(6)(C))
- Signing any document without an attorney present is extremely risky

**Due process in removal proceedings (INA §240):**
- Right to a hearing before an immigration judge before being removed (with limited exceptions)
- Right to present evidence and cross-examine witnesses
- Right to appeal to the Board of Immigration Appeals (BIA) within 30 days
- Right to further appeal to a federal circuit court of appeals

**Right to an attorney:**
- You have the right to hire a private immigration attorney at your own expense
- The government does not provide a free attorney in immigration court (unlike criminal court)
- If detained, you have the right to a bond hearing
- You have the right to contact your home country's consulate (Vienna Convention Article 36)

**Asylum rights (INA §208):**
- Any person physically present in the US may apply for asylum regardless of status
- Must file within 1 year of arrival unless a valid exception applies (changed/extraordinary circumstances)
- Cannot be returned to a country where life or freedom is threatened (non-refoulement)
- Withholding of removal (INA §241(b)(3)) and CAT (Convention Against Torture) are separate, stronger protections

**Special protections:**
- VAWA self-petitioners: confidential filing, DHS cannot share information with abuser
- U-Visa holders: protection from removal while petition is pending
- TPS holders: cannot be detained solely for immigration status while TPS is valid
- DACA recipients: deferred action means enforcement is deprioritized while status is valid
- Unaccompanied minors: entitled to counsel, cannot be placed in expedited removal

**If detained:**
- You have the right to a bond hearing before an immigration judge
- ICE must provide notice of the bond amount and you may request a hearing to lower it
- You have the right to make phone calls to an attorney and your consulate
- You cannot be held indefinitely without a hearing (Zadvydas v. Davis, 533 U.S. 678)

## Follow-up Questions

- Were you approached at home, at work, or in a public place?
- Did any officer ask you to sign anything or show you any paperwork? If so, what did it say?
- Are you currently detained or have you been released?
- Did officers enter your home — and if so, did they show you a warrant?
- Have you spoken to or been questioned by any immigration officer already?
- Do you have DACA, TPS, a U-Visa, or any pending USCIS application that may protect you?
